# **Topographical Analysis of High-Density UAV-LiDAR Datasets for Controlled Traffic Farming and Robotic Path Planning**

## **Introduction to Lidar-Derived Topologies in Precision Agriculture**

The intersection of high-resolution remote sensing and autonomous agricultural robotics has precipitated a fundamental shift in how broadacre farming environments are mapped, navigated, and managed. As agricultural rovers and unmanned ground vehicles (UGVs) become increasingly prevalent in modern cropping systems, the demand for precise, spatially accurate topographical data has grown exponentially.1 For autonomous path planners, the physical structure of the field—specifically the presence of compacted wheel tracks, varying crop canopy densities, and localized terrain deformation—dictates the kinematic feasibility and energetic cost of any given route.3 In order to deploy autonomous rovers efficiently, roboticists require comprehensive environmental models that capture the nuances of agricultural terrain.  
Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR), particularly when deployed via Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs), provides an unparalleled mechanism for capturing the three-dimensional geometry of agricultural landscapes.4 By emitting hundreds of thousands of laser pulses per second and measuring the time of flight of the returned energy, UAV-LiDAR systems can penetrate dense vegetative canopies to reveal the bare-earth topography beneath.5 This capability is uniquely critical in the context of Controlled Traffic Farming (CTF), an agronomic management strategy that confines all heavy machinery traffic to permanent, parallel wheel lanes.7 For a robotics path planner designed to prefer existing vehicle tracks over open ground, high-density point clouds serve as the foundational data structure from which cost-raster heuristics are derived.3  
The objective of this comprehensive analysis is to evaluate, categorize, and synthesize available UAV-LiDAR and high-density Airborne Laser Scanning (ALS) datasets that feature agricultural land, with a specific emphasis on the visibility of CTF infrastructure. By establishing a comparative baseline against state-of-the-art commercial sensors, such as the DJI Zenmuse L3, this report outlines the spatial resolution, classification accuracy, and geographical context required to successfully train and tune cost-raster algorithms for autonomous rover navigation. The subsequent sections will delve into the agronomic mechanics of CTF, the physics of LiDAR pulse propagation in vegetative canopies, algorithmic ground classification, and an exhaustive review of candidate datasets suitable for robotic path-planning development.

## **The Geometric and Structural Signatures of Controlled Traffic Farming**

To effectively tune a cost-raster heuristic to identify and prefer vehicle tracks, it is imperative to understand the physical and geometric signatures of Controlled Traffic Farming as they manifest in a point cloud.7 Traditional, unmanaged field traffic results in random, overlapping compaction zones that can cover up to eighty-five percent of a paddock's surface area, severely degrading soil health and increasing energy expenditure for subsequent operations.8 In contrast, CTF is a highly structured spatial management system that strictly separates the crop zone from the traffic zone.7

### **Agronomic Mechanics and Soil Deformation**

Under a CTF regime, the traffic zone (often referred to as tramlines or wheel tracks) typically occupies only ten to fifteen percent of the total field area.7 Because all machinery—from heavy combine harvesters to lighter spraying equipment—operates on the same permanent tracks year after year, these lanes become heavily compacted.8 Agronomically, this is highly beneficial; the uncompacted crop zone retains a porous structure that absorbs water, allowing for deep root penetration and minimizing water erosion, while reducing overall greenhouse gas emissions such as nitrous oxide and methane.7 Furthermore, the compacted traffic lanes provide a firm, stable surface that improves traction, reduces rolling resistance, and lowers machinery fuel consumption.2  
From a remote sensing and topographical perspective, this long-term compaction creates distinct, measurable depressions in the soil surface. A mature CTF tramline typically presents as a dual-rut system. Depending on the gauge width of the specific machinery, which relies on global positioning systems (GPS) for auto-guidance, these ruts are perfectly parallel and run the entire length of the paddock.1 In regions like Australia and New Zealand, where CTF is dominant in broadacre cropping, standardized gauges frequently sit at three-meter, nine-meter, or twelve-meter intervals.8 The ruts themselves are generally forty to sixty centimeters wide and can range from five to thirty centimeters deep, depending on soil properties, such as those found in swelling Black Vertosols or Red Chromosols, and the moisture conditions present during trafficking events.12

### **The Lidar Signature of Tramline Infrastructure**

When surveyed by a high-density LiDAR sensor, a CTF paddock yields a highly recognizable signature in the bare-earth point cloud. If the point density is sufficient, the digital terrain model (DTM) derived from the ground points will clearly show the parallel micro-depressions of the wheel ruts.11 Detecting these ruts, however, requires penetrating the crop canopy. In dense broadacre crops such as mature wheat, triticale, or cotton, the canopy can be effectively opaque to passive optical sensors, rendering photogrammetry ineffective for sub-canopy terrain modeling.15 While photogrammetry can map visible tracks in bare soil or early growth stages, it fails to resolve the underlying ruts once canopy closure occurs.2  
LiDAR circumvents this limitation through multiple discrete returns. A single laser pulse striking a canopy will scatter, allowing a fraction of the photons to continue downward. This allows the final returns of a single laser pulse to reach the bottom of the tramline rut, providing the critical elevation data necessary to model the terrain.5 For an autonomous rover path planner, these lidar-derived ruts are the ideal navigation corridors. Treating the CTF tracks as low-cost edges in a spatial graph allows algorithms to generate routes that minimize energy expenditure, avoid damaging delicate crop zones, and utilize the enhanced traction of the compacted soil.3

## **Sensor Paradigms: Establishing the DJI Zenmuse L3 Baseline**

Evaluating candidate datasets requires a thorough understanding of the sensor technology used to acquire them. The reference baseline—a sample from the DJI Zenmuse L3 processed by ROCK Robotic—represents a modern, prosumer-to-commercial grade UAV-LiDAR workflow.19 The specifications of this sensor define the target parameters for any comparable dataset utilized in the robotic path-planning pipeline.

### **The Evolution of UAV-LiDAR Payloads**

The DJI Zenmuse series has evolved rapidly, fundamentally democratizing access to aerial LiDAR for agricultural applications. Early iterations, such as the Zenmuse L1, utilized a Livox Avia sensor, providing a cost-effective entry point for aerial mapping.20 However, the L1 was limited by its spot size and penetration capabilities in extremely dense vegetation.22 The Zenmuse L2 introduced significant improvements, featuring a thirty percent increase in range and a substantially tighter spot size of 4 × 12 centimeters at one hundred meters, which is roughly one-fifth the spot size of the L1.22 This tighter beam divergence allowed the L2 to slip between leaves and branches more effectively, generating more accurate ground points beneath dense canopies.22  
The reference Zenmuse L3 dataset represents a further refinement in this lineage. Operating at a typical flight altitude of approximately 103.5 meters above ground level and a flight speed of 7.55 meters per second, the L3 can achieve average point densities of 349 points per square meter.19 Crucially, the L3 supports up to seven returns per pulse.17 In agricultural settings, high return counts are vital for penetrating structural complexity. A laser pulse striking a mature wheat canopy will scatter; the first return may trigger on the wheat awns, the second and third on the mid-canopy leaves, and only the fifth, sixth, or seventh return may possess enough residual energy to reflect off the soil at the bottom of a CTF rut.6 Furthermore, the dataset integrates RGB colorization, assigning true-color values to individual points, which greatly assists in the visual verification of tracks and the development of multimodal semantic segmentation networks.19

## **Point Cloud Densities and Spatial Resolution Requirements for Rut Detection**

The efficacy of a cost-raster heuristic relies entirely on the spatial resolution of the underlying point cloud. The reference Zenmuse L3 dataset provides approximately 335 points per square meter, serving as a highly effective Tier A threshold.19 Understanding why this density is advantageous—and determining the minimum acceptable density for Tier B fallback datasets—requires an application of the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem to spatial topographies.

### **Spatial Sampling Theory in Agricultural Geomorphology**

If a CTF wheel rut is approximately thirty to forty centimeters wide, spatial sampling theory dictates that the terrain must be sampled at least twice within that spatial frequency to reliably detect the feature without aliasing. Therefore, a ground point is required every fifteen centimeters at a minimum. A perfect grid of points spaced fifteen centimeters apart equates to approximately 44.4 points per square meter.  
However, this theoretical minimum assumes a perfectly even distribution of points and a completely bare earth surface. In reality, UAV-LiDAR point distribution is semi-stochastic, influenced by the scanning pattern (e.g., non-repetitive rosette patterns or parallel line scans), aircraft roll, pitch, and yaw, and high-frequency platform vibration.6 More importantly, crop canopy occlusion dictates that only a fraction of the emitted pulses will ever reach the ground.6 If a dense wheat crop intercepts eighty percent of the laser pulses, a raw emission density of 200 points per square meter will yield a ground point density of only 40 points per square meter, perilously close to the absolute theoretical limit for detecting a 30-centimeter feature.  
Consequently, a raw density of 100 points per square meter is generally considered the operational minimum for reliable CTF rut detection under partial canopy, defining the Tier A dataset category. Datasets with densities greater than or equal to 10 points per square meter (Tier B datasets) are acceptable fallback options, particularly if the data was captured during a "leaf-off" or post-harvest state. During these periods, residual dry matter or short stubble presents minimal canopy occlusion, allowing nearly all emitted pulses to strike the bare earth and accurately map the tramlines.33

## **Algorithmic Processing: Ground Classification and Bare-Earth Extraction**

The reference dataset specifies a clear separation between ground (Class 2\) and unclassified points \[prompt\]. For a rover path planner, the accurate classification of bare earth is arguably the most critical preprocessing step. The American Society for Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing (ASPRS) defines standard LiDAR point classes, where Class 2 is universally reserved for bare-earth ground points, while classes 3, 4, and 5 delineate low, medium, and high vegetation respectively.29 If crop stalks are falsely classified as ground, the resulting digital elevation model will exhibit high artificial roughness, deterring the path planner from valid routes. Conversely, if the actual terrain features, such as the steep edges of a deep CTF rut, are filtered out and misclassified as vegetation or noise, the planner will fail to recognize the optimal traffic lanes.36

### **Cloth Simulation Filtering and Morphological Approaches**

Standard LiDAR processing pipelines utilize various algorithms to extract the bare earth from raw point clouds. The Cloth Simulation Filter (CSF) algorithm is widely utilized in agricultural and forestry contexts.24 CSF operates by mathematically inverting the point cloud and simulating a rigid cloth falling onto the inverted points. The physical stiffness parameters of the simulated cloth prevent it from sagging into deep spikes, which correspond to tall vegetation in the un-inverted cloud, allowing it to rest smoothly on the macroscopic terrain surface.36  
In CTF environments, the parameters of the ground classification algorithm must be tuned with extreme precision. The tramline ruts represent sudden, sharp depressions in the local topography. If a filtering algorithm is too aggressive or assumes a uniformly smooth field, it may interpret the bottom of a wheel rut as a multi-path error or a low-noise spike, effectively stripping the rut from the Class 2 ground model and replacing it with interpolated data.11 Datasets that have been professionally processed by state agencies or premier research institutions typically utilize localized adaptive filtering to preserve these critical micro-topographical features.29 For datasets lacking pre-classification (Class 0), roboticists must implement their own filtering pipelines using tools like PDAL or LASTools before cost-raster generation can begin.

## **Autonomous Rover Path Planning: Integrating Topographic Cost Rasters**

The transition from a raw, classified LAZ file to an actionable path-planning framework requires the generation of a specialized cost raster. In the context of a robotics capstone, the goal is to compute routes that heavily penalize open crop ground while aggressively rewarding travel along existing CTF vehicle tracks \[prompt\]. This involves translating the discrete 3D points of a Class 2 point cloud into a continuous 2D grid, evaluating the heuristic cost of each cell based on its topographical properties.

### **Heuristic Generation and Cost Surface Anisotropy**

Once the ground points are isolated, the point cloud is rasterized into a high-resolution Digital Elevation Model (DEM).39 To isolate the wheel tracks, secondary topographic indices are derived from the DEM.

1. **Terrain Roughness and Slope:** CTF tracks are designed to be smooth and strictly parallel to the field's dominant orientation to maximize machinery efficiency.8 By calculating localized terrain roughness, the tracks will appear as linear zones of low elevation variance amidst the higher variance of the natural soil clods and crop residue in the inter-row zones.  
2. **Topographic Position Index (TPI):** TPI compares the elevation of a specific cell to the mean elevation of a specified surrounding neighborhood. Because tramline ruts are depressed by five to thirty centimeters relative to the adjacent crop beds 12, a TPI calculation with a radial neighborhood matching the machinery gauge width will dramatically highlight the wheel tracks as negative anomalies.

The cost raster heuristic is then defined by mathematically weighting and combining these layers. The resulting cost surface is highly anisotropic. The cost of a rover moving longitudinally parallel to the CTF tracks, remaining inside the rut, is exceptionally low. Conversely, the cost of traversing perpendicularly across the tracks, which involves climbing out of a rut, moving across the uncompacted crop zone, and dropping into the next rut, incurs a massive algorithmic penalty.3

### **The Fields2Cover Paradigm and Kinematic Constraints**

Modern frameworks such as the open-source Fields2Cover library are actively utilized to solve these agricultural Coverage Path Planning (CPP) problems.3 While traditional CPP algorithms often focus purely on minimizing total route length or operational time, modern agricultural planners integrate soil compaction models, like the SoilFlex model, to actively route robots along pre-existing swaths.3  
By ingesting the lidar-derived cost rasters discussed in this report, a path planner can dynamically adapt its behavior to the terrain. This is particularly crucial during headland turning maneuvers. When a rover reaches the end of a tramline, it must execute a turn to enter the next swath. Fields2Cover and similar algorithms account for the robot's kinematic constraints, such as minimum turning radius and curvature change rates, to generate headland paths that minimize soil damage.3 By coupling these kinematic constraints with a LiDAR-derived cost raster, the path planner ensures the rover remains within the bounds of the compacted soil, effectively preventing damage to the highly productive, uncompacted crop zones.3

## **Global Repositories and Open-Data Architectures for Agricultural Topography**

Sourcing datasets comparable to the Zenmuse L3 requires navigating a fragmented landscape of academic, governmental, and commercial data repositories. The geographical priority for CTF data naturally falls to Australia and New Zealand, regions that have pioneered and heavily adopted broadacre CTF to manage fragile soil profiles, prevent compaction, and preserve scarce moisture.8

### **Australian and New Zealand Data Infrastructure**

Australia and New Zealand offer some of the most robust and accessible open-spatial data infrastructures globally, making them ideal hunting grounds for agricultural LiDAR.

* **LINZ (Land Information New Zealand):** The Toitū Te Whenua LINZ National Elevation Programme is an exemplary resource. LINZ has partnered with regional councils, such as Environment Canterbury, to capture immense swathes of the country using high-end ALS systems.40 The datasets covering the Canterbury Plains—a massive agricultural hub—comprise tens of thousands of square kilometers of LiDAR at impressive ALS densities, providing a macro-scale view of CTF adoption across thousands of individual paddocks.29  
* **CSIRO Data Access Portal (DAP):** The Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation (CSIRO) serves as Australia's premier scientific repository. The DAP hosts highly specific, academic-grade agricultural datasets. Notable among these are the Multi-Modality Dataset for Crop Biomass Estimation (MMCBE) and various wheat and triticale plot trials at Yanco, New South Wales.16 These datasets often feature extremely high-density UAV or terrestrial LiDAR point clouds, meticulously curated for precision agriculture research and phenotyping.15  
* **ELVIS (Elevation and Depth \- Foundation Spatial Data):** Managed by Geoscience Australia, ELVIS federates elevation data across Australian states and territories.44 While much of the baseline data is provided as a 5-meter grid resolution DEM, localized high-density point clouds from state initiatives, such as NSW SPADE, can occasionally be sourced here, particularly those associated with flood-plain or riverbank erosion-monitoring projects that intersect agricultural land.39

### **International Repositories and Vendor Archives**

Beyond the Antipodes, platforms like OpenTopography host a diverse array of community and government data. OpenTopography frequently mirrors LINZ data but also hosts localized high-density UAV flights uploaded directly by academic institutions through their Community Dataspace.46 For example, studies in California assessing Residual Dry Matter (RDM) provide exceptional density over pasture land, illustrating the extreme spatial resolutions achievable with low-altitude UAV-LiDAR.48  
Furthermore, commercial sensor vendors, including YellowScan and Phoenix LiDAR Systems, maintain public galleries of sample data. These datasets are intended to showcase sensor capabilities to prospective buyers and are often perfectly aligned with the Zenmuse L3 reference specifications, featuring high point densities, multiple returns, and standard LAS or LAZ formatting.28

## **Exhaustive Review of Candidate Point Cloud Datasets**

To fulfill the requirements of the robotics path-planning pipeline, a rigorous selection of candidate datasets has been identified from the aforementioned repositories. These datasets span both Tier A, representing ultra-high density UAV-LiDAR, and Tier B, representing high-density ALS, ensuring a broad spectrum of training data for cost-raster tuning and validation.

### **1\. The MuST-C Dataset (University of Bonn)**

The Multi-Sensor, multi-Temporal, multiple Crops (MuST-C) dataset is a paramount resource for agricultural robotics and phenotyping.25 Collected at the Campus Klein-Altendorf research facility at the University of Bonn in Germany, this dataset was specifically designed to overcome the phenotyping bottleneck in precision agriculture through automation.51

* **Sensor and Density:** Data was acquired using a RIEGL miniVUX-SYS LiDAR mounted on a DJI Matrice 600 PRO UAV.25 After filtering the data to a maximum range of forty-five meters, the resulting georeferenced point cloud achieved a staggering mean density of 1,433 points per square meter.25 The dataset also includes terrestrial data from an Ouster OS1 multi-beam LiDAR featuring 128 beams.25  
* **Characteristics:** The dataset covers multiple crop types, including sugar beet, spring wheat, sweet corn, soybean, and potato, across 7.5 by 6-meter experimental plots.25 Crucially for rover path planning, the dataset includes Unmanned Ground Vehicle (UGV) traverses generated by a Thorvald II robot.25 The data is provided in standard LAS format, though ground classification algorithms must be applied independently.25  
* **Utility:** At over 1400 points per square meter, the spatial resolution vastly exceeds the L3 baseline. The presence of actual UGV tracks navigating through varied crop canopies makes this the ultimate analog for rover path-planning algorithms, allowing developers to test how well their heuristics detect wheel ruts generated by machinery of a similar scale to their own rovers.

### **2\. CSIRO MMCBE (Cotton) and Yanco (Wheat/Triticale)**

The CSIRO Data Access Portal provides two highly relevant point cloud datasets focused on broadacre cropping in Australia.

* **MMCBE Dataset:** The Multi-Modality Dataset for Crop Biomass Estimation comprises 216 sets of multi-view drone images coupled with high-density LiDAR point clouds.16 Focused on cotton crops, which are quintessential broadacre row crops often grown under strict CTF regimes, the data was collected at ultra-low altitudes of twelve meters above ground level at a speed of 1.5 meters per second using a DJI M300.16 The resulting density provides centimeter-level 3D reconstructions of the crop and underlying soil surface.6  
* **Yanco Field Trials (csiro:52108):** Collected in New South Wales in 2019, this dataset features LiDAR scans of wheat and triticale plots.38 The plots were sown at varying densities to deliberately encompass diverse canopy architectures and aboveground biomass values.38  
* **Utility:** These datasets are ideal Tier A candidates. They mirror the DJI ecosystem of the L3 reference sample and provide the extremely dense point clouds necessary to analyze how laser pulses penetrate grassy or shrubby canopies to reach the soil surface.16 The structural data of the cotton rows and inter-row spaces will severely test the anisotropic weighting of any path planner.

### **3\. OpenTopography: Canterbury, New Zealand 2020-2025**

This dataset represents the premier Tier B option. Managed by LINZ and hosted on OpenTopography, it covers the vast agricultural expanses of the Canterbury Plains.29

* **Sensor and Density:** Captured using an Optech Galaxy PRIME ALS system flown at over 2800 meters above mean ground level, the system utilized a pulse rate of 400 kilohertz and a fifty-five percent swath overlap.29 Despite the high altitude, the system achieved a remarkable point density of 11.90 points per square meter.29  
* **Characteristics:** The dataset encompasses over 34,223 square kilometers and contains more than 407 billion points delivered in compressed LAZ format.29 The data is rigorously classified according to ASPRS standards, with over 135 billion points explicitly identified as Class 2 (Ground).29 It utilizes the NZTM2000 coordinate reference system.29  
* **Utility:** The Canterbury Plains are a global stronghold for Controlled Traffic Farming. Exploring the Class 2 points in this dataset reveals tens of thousands of hectares of striking, parallel CTF tramlines.1 For a robotics student, this dataset is invaluable for testing whether a path planner's cost heuristics can successfully scale from a forty-nine-hectare sample to a macro-regional topography.

### **4\. Commercial Samples: YellowScan Mapper+ and Phoenix LiDAR**

Commercial LiDAR vendors provide open sample datasets that match or exceed the L3 specifications, serving as excellent standalone testing environments.

* **YellowScan Mapper+ (Scarborough, UK):** This sample was flown over "Forestry \- Farm land" at 80 meters above ground level and a flight speed of 8 meters per second.28 Delivered in LAS format, it highlights the capabilities of the Mapper+ system, which boasts multiple returns capable of penetrating vegetation in agricultural settings.28  
* **Phoenix LiDAR Ranger-U240 (Big Sandy Creek):** Phoenix provides sample datasets of river corridors and flood-impacted land.50 The Ranger-U240 dataset over Big Sandy Creek, while not exclusively CTF farmland, achieved 130 points per square meter.53 This high density provides excellent insights into ground terrain modeling under riparian and adjacent agricultural vegetation, testing the limits of bare-earth filtering algorithms.53

### **5\. Academic CTF Studies: Macak et al. (Central Europe)**

A 2023 study by Macak et al. specifically investigated the effects of Controlled Traffic Farming on water erosion on steep land.11

* **Characteristics:** The research produced a highly detailed digital terrain model and point cloud of a 16-hectare experimental field in Slovakia.11 The spatial resolution of the point cloud is reported at 0.03 meters, representing immense spatial fidelity.11  
* **Utility:** Because the core focus of the paper is the physical impact of CTF, the topographical data inherently features distinct, permanent wheel tracks.54 This dataset serves as a perfect European counterpart to the Australian and New Zealand CTF norms, ensuring that the path planner's heuristics are not overfitted to Southern Hemisphere soil mechanics.

### **6\. OpenTopography: Dawson Los Monos Canyon Reserve Pasture**

As an example of extreme high-density UAV mapping, this pilot study in California assessed Residual Dry Matter in rangeland pastures.48

* **Sensor and Density:** Flown at varying altitudes of 60 meters and 120 meters above ground level, the dataset achieved an overall average density of 1,876.94 points per square meter across a 12-hectare area.48  
* **Characteristics:** The data is provided in LAZ format in the WGS 84 / UTM zone 11N coordinate system. Notably, the points are entirely unclassified, remaining in Class 0\.48  
* **Utility:** While it lacks pre-computed bare-earth extraction, the exorbitant point density makes it an excellent candidate for testing custom cloth-simulation ground filtering scripts before generating cost rasters.36 Testing filtering algorithms on datasets with nearly 2000 points per square meter ensures that the algorithms do not mistakenly remove the micro-topographical depressions of vehicle tracks during the smoothing process.

To optimize the path-planning pipeline, prioritize the **MuST-C Dataset** and the **Canterbury LINZ** dataset. MuST-C provides the exact ultra-high-density UAV-scale mechanics required, complete with explicit UGV wheel tracks navigating through crop plots, making it the perfect micro-scale tuning environment. Conversely, the Canterbury LINZ dataset provides the ultimate macro-scale testbed; despite being ALS, the 11.9 pts/m² density over 34,000 km² of New Zealand broadacre farming will validate whether cost-raster heuristics scale to massive, real-world CTF operations.  
An obvious blind spot in this search is proprietary European and North American corporate ag-tech data; immense volumes of high-density UAV-LiDAR CTF data are held by precision agriculture consultancies (e.g., John Deere, Trimble) but are strictly paywalled or restricted to client use. Finally, a non-obvious category worth a deeper, separate search is archaeological LiDAR archives (e.g., historic UK Environment Agency scans). Archaeological scans often utilize extreme bare-earth filtering to find ancient earthworks, and these algorithms inadvertently expose modern tractor tramlines with startling clarity, providing ready-made test environments for track-following heuristics.
